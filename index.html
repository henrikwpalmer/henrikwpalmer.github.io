COVID Quaranspace

Date: April 2020
Medium/Tools: Processing, p5.js, Run Trainer App
Overview: COVID Quaranspace is designed as a way for users to use geolocation to develop form and reflect on their daily life patters. Intially created as an application, the project consists of a window for uploading screenshots of running or walking paths recorded on a running tracker which are then manipulated and orbited in black space.

Code Description: The code employs a Java script library for importing images from the user. The uploaded image is then processed pixel-by-pixel to identify the pixels showing the path taken. All other pixels are made transluscent thereby removing excess noise in the screenshot. The geographic center of the path is calculated and is used as the folcrum for rotating in 3D space. Each uploaded image is assigned a random color based on a predetermined color pallette

Concept: COVID Quaranspace was being developed as I was contemplating the emergent powers claimed by the state during what Carl Schmitt deems the "state of emergency." During this period of heightened national anxiety, governments had or claimed license to impose strict social isoloation standards, shortcut data security protections, and fast-track legislation. Of course, chief among these was the lockdown protocols that many of the world's governments were implementing to varying degrees of enforcement in order to "slow the spread."

The application reflects on the availability of geolocation data and how these data might be used to map infection risk, social interaction, and social isolation. Further iterations would have incorporated connectivity between users such that users could see how their Quaranspace did or did not overlap with others in their community.

Hope Dandelion AR Filter

Date: May 2020
Medium/Tools: SparkAR Studio
Overview: Hope Dandelion is an augmented reality (AR) filter made available on Instagram's filter library that invites users to momentarily inhabit a pre-industrial ecological worldâ€”one in which the American Chestnut tree still flourished. By blowing on the non-native dandelion, users unlock a transformation of their surroundings, exploring a dream-like world of abundant fruits and vegetation.

Experience: The filter opens with the user in selfie view, holding a digital dandelion with the prompt to make a wish. After blowing on the seeds, blossoms begin to floatâ€”first apple and peach blossoms, then chestnutsâ€”evoking a Jennifer Steinkamp-like unreality. Switching to the back camera reveals a fully rendered American Chestnut tree in bloom. Blossoms and chestnuts drift through the augmented space, which can be explored from all angles.

Concept: The project was developed during the COVID-19 pandemic and draws parallels between human and ecological disease. It reflects on the cascading pandemics currently affecting New England forests where I grew upâ€”from the Hemlock Woolly Adelgid to the Emerald Ash Borer and the Dutch Elm Diseaseâ€”and mourns the disappearance of species long snuffed out. The American Chestnut, once central to Applachian life as a source of communal food and economic goods, was decimated by a blight imported via global trade- the same force that amplified its economic value.

By using a Eurasian dandelion as the symbolic key to unlock this imagined past, Hope Dandelion explores the contradictions inherent to economic prosperity enabled through environmental degradation, themes of collective memory and loss, and interconnectivity in a globalized world. It asks: what does restoration look like when it carries the seeds of further deterioration?

Conjoined Solids

The goal of this project was to arrange three found, wooden blocks using a 3D printer. I further defined an objective to connect these volumes by placing a vertex of one object, with a surface of the next. I therefore positioned the blocks in this way and used Rhino modeling software to create planar objects that froze this orientation. In order to maintain this planar component and accent the competing planes in the blocks, I expanded the joints with parabolic shapes. The large flat surface of the joints made printing more difficult, however, in both cases I used the 3D printer effectively and got well produced results.
