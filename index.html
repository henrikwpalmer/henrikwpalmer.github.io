COVID Quaranspace

Date: April 2020
Medium/Tools: Processing, p5.js, Drop Library, Run Trainer App
Description: COVID Quaranspace is designed as a way for users to use geolocation to develop form. Intially created as an application, the sketch consists of a window for uploading screenshots of running or walking paths recorded on a running tracker. The code performs pixels operations on uploaded screenshots to identify the paths taken while making transluscent excess noise in the screenshot. The geographic center of the loop or form is caluculated and is used as the axis for rotating in 3D space.

At the onset of the pandemic, the piece grapples with confinement that lots of the world was experiencing as lockdown measures were being implemented to "slow the spread." Further iterations would have incorporated connectivity between users such that users could see how their Quaranspace did or did not overlap with others in their community.

Hope Dandelion AR Filter

Date: May 2020
Medium/Tools: SparkAR Studio
Description: The Hope Dandelion AR Filter was developed as a way for entering a world prior to the environmental degredation brought on by industrialization, that is, a world in which the American Chestnut Tree still blumed in North America. The user finds themself in the font-facing camera view with the instruction to make a wish on a Dandelion. After blowing on the Dandelion, a Laurie Anderson-like world unfolds first with apple and peach blossoms, then with chestnuts floating in augmented reality space. After switching to the back camera, the user finds an American Chestnut tree in blume before them. The tree can be explored on the inside and out as blossoms and chestnuts continue to spawn. Throughout 2020, Hope Dandelion was available for use through Instagram's filter library.

The project was spurred by the many pandemics that affect not only human life, but also the natural world. Conjoined Solids

The goal of this project was to arrange three found, wooden blocks using a 3D printer. I further defined an objective to connect these volumes by placing a vertex of one object, with a surface of the next. I therefore positioned the blocks in this way and used Rhino modeling software to create planar objects that froze this orientation. In order to maintain this planar component and accent the competing planes in the blocks, I expanded the joints with parabolic shapes. The large flat surface of the joints made printing more difficult, however, in both cases I used the 3D printer effectively and got well produced results.
